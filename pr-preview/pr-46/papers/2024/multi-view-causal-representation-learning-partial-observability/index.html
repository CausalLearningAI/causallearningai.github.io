Multi-View Causal Representation Learning with Partial Observability

A unified identifiability framework: any information shared across any subset of partial views is recoverable up to a smooth bijection with contrastive learning

Causal Representation Learning
Identifiability
Theory

This work presents a unified framework for the identifiability of representations learned from simultaneously observed views — different data modalities or measurements — under partial observability, where each view is a nonlinear mixture of only a subset of underlying, possibly causally related, latent variables. The information shared across all subsets of any number of views is shown to be recoverable up to a smooth bijection using contrastive learning and a single encoder per view. Simple graphical rules — an “identifiability algebra” — then say exactly which latents can be isolated. The framework unifies and extends prior work on multi-view nonlinear ICA, disentanglement, and causal representation learning, and is validated on numerical, image, and multi-modal data.

Overview

Many scientific problems share a structure: a phenomenon is governed by a small set of latent factors, but every instrument only measures an entangled, nonlinear view of some of them. A patient may undergo several medical exams — an X-ray, an MRI, a blood test — each shedding light on part of their overall health, none offering a comprehensive picture. Causal representation learning (Schölkopf et al. 2021) asks when those latent factors can be recovered, relaxing the assumption of independence central to nonlinear ICA. When mixing functions are nonlinear, recovering the latents from a single view is generally impossible (Locatello et al. 2019); the community has therefore turned to multi-view settings, where several observations refer to the same realization of the latents.

Recovering nonlinear ICA’s independence assumption is exactly the “independent” in ICA — causal representation learning drops it.
Schölkopf, Bernhard, Francesco Locatello, Stefan Bauer, et al. 2021. “Toward Causal Representation Learning.” Proceedings of the IEEE 109 (5): 612–34.
Gresele, Luigi, Paul K. Rubenstein, Arash Mehrjou, Francesco Locatello, and Bernhard Schölkopf. 2020. “The Incomplete Rosetta Stone Problem: Identifiability Results for Multi-View Nonlinear ICA.” Uncertainty in Artificial Intelligence, 217–27.
Ahuja, Kartik, Jason S. Hartford, and Yoshua Bengio. 2022. “Weakly Supervised Representation Learning with Sparse Perturbations.” Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 35: 15516–28.

This paper gives a unified identifiability framework for observational multi-view causal representation learning under partial observability: views need not be functions of all the latents, only of some, and the number of views is arbitrary. With a single proof, the result implies the identifiability of several prior works — multi-view causal representation learning (Kügelgen et al. 2021; Daunhawer et al. 2023), multi-view nonlinear ICA (Gresele et al. 2020), and weakly-supervised disentanglement (Locatello et al. 2020; Ahuja et al. 2022) — as special cases.

The setting: partial views of shared latents

Let \mathbf{z}=(\mathbf{z}_1,\dots,\mathbf{z}_N)\sim p_{\mathbf{z}} be possibly dependent (causally related) latent variables. Rather than observing \mathbf{z}, we observe K entangled views \mathbf{x}=(\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_K), where each view depends only on a subset of the latents — its view-specific latents \mathbf{z}_{S_k}, indexed by S_k\subseteq[N] — through a smooth, invertible view-specific mixing function f_k:

\mathbf{z}\sim p_{\mathbf{z}}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_k := f_k(\mathbf{z}_{S_k}) \quad \forall k\in[K].

For any subset of views V_i, the content is the set of latents shared by all of them — the intersection of their indexing sets, C_i=\bigcap_{k\in V_i} S_k — and the remaining latents are style, relative to that subset. Unlike some prior work, here style is not discarded: it may itself be of interest and can sometimes be identified by other means.

In the running example (Figure 1) with K=4 views and N=6 latents, the content of the pair (\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2) is (\mathbf{z}_1,\mathbf{z}_2,\mathbf{z}_3,\mathbf{z}_5), while the content shared by all four views is just (\mathbf{z}_1,\mathbf{z}_2). The goal is to recover each such content block up to a smooth invertible reparameterization — the notion of block-identifiability introduced by Kügelgen et al. (2021) and here restated for the multi-view, multi-modal setting: an encoder block-identifies the content if it captures all and only the information in those latents.

Identifiability theory

The first result establishes when the shared content of a set of views can be learned. Given content encoders g_k mapping each observation to the unit cube, consider the objective

\mathcal{L}(G)= \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{k<k'}\mathbb{E}\!\left[\,\lVert g_k(\mathbf{x}_k)-g_{k'}(\mathbf{x}_{k'})\rVert_2\,\right]}_{\text{content alignment}} -\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{k} H\!\left(g_k(\mathbf{x}_k)\right)}_{\text{entropy regularization}},

where H(\cdot) is differential entropy. Minimizing it block-identifies the shared content \mathbf{z}_C. Intuitively, the alignment term forces the encoders to keep only the shared content and discard the styles, while the entropy term pushes towards a uniform — hence invertible — representation. This extends prior results that were limited to two views (Kügelgen et al. 2021; Daunhawer et al. 2023; Locatello et al. 2020) to an arbitrary number.

Uniformity implies invertibility of the encoders, which is what block-identifiability requires.

Recovering every content block this way would require training a separate set of encoders for each subset of views — exponentially many. The paper avoids this by learning a single view-specific encoder r_k per view, paired with lightweight binary content selectors that pick out, from one shared representation, the latents belonging to each content block. Reconciling disentanglement (which the shared representation should promote) with the uniformity needed for invertibility requires auxiliary projection functions, used purely as a theoretical device so that invertibility can be shown without forcing the learned latents to be independent.

Identifiability algebra

Once several content blocks are identified, simple graphical rules — what the authors call the identifiability algebra — say what else follows. For two identified blocks \mathbf{z}_{C_1} and \mathbf{z}_{C_2}:

  • Intersection. \mathbf{z}_{C_1\cap C_2} can always be block-identified.
  • Complement. If C_1\cap C_2 is independent of C_1\setminus C_2, then \mathbf{z}_{C_1\setminus C_2} can be block-identified.
  • Union. If C_1\cap C_2, C_1\setminus C_2 and C_2\setminus C_1 are mutually independent, then \mathbf{z}_{C_1\cup C_2} can be block-identified.

Applied iteratively to a qualitative graphical description of the data-generating process (as in Figure 1), these rules determine exactly which latents — or groups of them — can be recovered and disentangled, subject to the technical assumptions of the main theorems.

Experiments

The claims are validated on a fully controlled numerical setup and on image and multi-modal data. Representations are evaluated by predicting the ground-truth latents from the learned ones — kernel ridge regression for continuous latents, logistic regression for discrete ones — and reporting the coefficient of determination R^2; a value near one indicates block-identifiability. The objectives are estimated with InfoNCE (Oord et al. 2018) and BarlowTwins (Zbontar et al. 2021).

Oord, Aaron van den, Yazhe Li, and Oriol Vinyals. 2018. “Representation Learning with Contrastive Predictive Coding.” arXiv Preprint arXiv:1807.03748.
Zbontar, Jure, Li Jing, Ishan Misra, Yann LeCun, and Stéphane Deny. 2021. “Barlow Twins: Self-Supervised Learning via Redundancy Reduction.” arXiv Preprint arXiv:2103.03230.

Theory validation (numerical). With four views generated from synthetic latents through randomly initialized invertible MLPs, the content variables are consistently identified (R^2\approx 1) as more views are added, while independent style variables remain non-predictable (R^2\approx 0) — the learned representation explains essentially all variation in the content block and nothing from the independent styles.

Self-supervised disentanglement (image). On two image datasets, the encoder-only method is compared against the autoencoder-based Ada-GVAE (Locatello et al. 2020, 2019) using DCI disentanglement. Avoiding image reconstruction, the contrastive method substantially improves disentanglement on visually complex data.

Locatello, Francesco, Ben Poole, Gunnar Raetsch, Bernhard Schölkopf, Olivier Bachem, and Michael Tschannen. 2020. “Weakly-Supervised Disentanglement Without Compromises.” Proceedings of the 37th International Conference on Machine Learning, Proceedings of machine learning research, vol. 119: 6348–59.
Locatello, Francesco, Stefan Bauer, Mario Lucic, et al. 2019. “Challenging Common Assumptions in the Unsupervised Learning of Disentangled Representations.” International Conference on Machine Learning, 4114–24.

Multi-modal partial observability. With three views — two images and one text caption — the text carries modality-specific factors (e.g. text phrasing) absent from any image, giving genuine partial observability. A single training run with view-specific encoders learns all content blocks simultaneously and recovers the performance of two separate prior setups — the image-pair setting of Kügelgen et al. (2021) and the image–caption setting of Daunhawer et al. (2023) — verifying the efficiency of the simultaneous formulation.

Kügelgen, Julius von, Yash Sharma, Luigi Gresele, et al. 2021. “Self-Supervised Learning with Data Augmentations Provably Isolates Content from Style.” Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 34: 16451–67.
Daunhawer, Imant, Alice Bizeul, Emanuele Palumbo, Alexander Marx, and Julia E. Vogt. 2023. “Identifiability Results for Multimodal Contrastive Learning.” The Eleventh International Conference on Learning Representations.
Lachapelle, Sébastien, Tristan Deleu, Divyat Mahajan, et al. 2023. “Synergies Between Disentanglement and Sparsity: Generalization and Identifiability in Multi-Task Learning.” International Conference on Machine Learning, 18171–206.
Fumero, Marco, Florian Wenzel, Luca Zancato, et al. 2023. Leveraging Sparse and Shared Feature Activations for Disentangled Representation Learning.

Multi-task disentanglement. Following the running example with latent causal relations, the learned representation eases a downstream classification task, reaching an accuracy of 0.99 with both linear and nonlinear labeling functions within 1k update steps — even though the latents are not independent. This explains the strong empirical performance of prior multi-task methods (Lachapelle et al. 2023; Fumero et al. 2023) on real data where the independence assumptions underlying their theory are likely violated.

Takeaways

Access to multiple partial views enables a more fine-grained representation under the generally milder assumption of partial observability. A single identifiability proof unifies multi-view nonlinear ICA, disentanglement, and causal representation learning, recovering each as a special case; the identifiability algebra turns a qualitative graph of the data-generating process into a concrete account of what is recoverable. The authors are explicit about the remaining gaps between theory and practice — notably discrete variables and finite-sample effects, which they address only empirically — and point to discovering hidden causal structure from overlapping, non-simultaneous marginals as open future work.
