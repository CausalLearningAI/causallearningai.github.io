The Rate-Distortion-Polysemanticity Tradeoff in SAEs

Why interpretable features cost rate and reconstruction

Interpretability
Theory

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) that accurately reconstruct their input (low distortion) while using few active features (low rate) often fail to learn monosemantic, human-interpretable features. We cast this tension as a rate-distortion-polysemanticity (RDP) tradeoff and show, theoretically and empirically, that enforcing monosemanticity raises both the rate and the distortion an SAE must pay. The optimal degree of polysemanticity is set by the data distribution — specifically by how ground-truth concepts co-occur — rather than by optimization choices. We derive necessary conditions that any polysemanticity measure must satisfy in realistic settings and audit nine proxies used on LLM-trained SAEs, finding that most fail to track the RDP-predicted direction. Polysemanticity is therefore a data-level constraint, not merely a training artifact.

Step 02 — Monosemantic: one feature, one concept Insist that each feature track exactly one ground-truth concept. Step 03 — Polysemantic: spend less rate Now let a single feature carry several co-occurring concepts at once. Packing cat, dog, fur into one unit keeps the reconstruction faithful (D = D_0) while fewer features fire — R \ll R_0. Interpretability is the price.

Step 04 — Polysemantic: spend less distortion The same packing trick can instead buy fidelity: hold the rate at R = R_0 and the freed capacity drives distortion down, D \ll D_0. Either way the lesson is one curve — enforcing monosemanticity lifts the whole frontier, so holding R or D fixed forces the other budget up.

Introduction

Sparse autoencoders have become the workhorse of mechanistic interpretability: trained to reconstruct a model’s activations through a sparse bottleneck, they promise to recover the underlying features a network uses (Cunningham et al. 2023). The hope is that these features are monosemantic — each one tracking a single human-legible concept rather than a blend of several (Bricken et al. 2023). In practice SAEs that reconstruct well with few active units tend to learn polysemantic features, and tightening one objective seems to slacken another.

We argue this is not an accident of training but an information-theoretic constraint. Borrowing the rate-distortion-perception framing of lossy compression (Blau and Michaeli 2019; Shannon 1959), we add a third axis — polysemanticity — and show the three quantities trade off against each other.

Superposition predicts that a model packs more concepts than it has dimensions, which is exactly what forces polysemantic codes.

A rate-distortion-polysemanticity formulation

An SAE encodes an input x to a sparse code z_\theta(x) and decodes back to \hat{x}_\theta. Training minimizes reconstruction error under a sparsity penalty S with weight \lambda:

\arg\min_{\theta\in\Theta}\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,\lVert x - \hat{x}_\theta\rVert_2^2 + \lambda\, S\!\left(z_\theta(x)\right)\right].

We summarize a trained SAE by three scalars. The rate R(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\,\lVert z_\theta(x)\rVert_0 counts active features; the distortion D(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\,\lVert x-\hat{x}_\theta\rVert_2^2 is the reconstruction error; and the polysemanticity of a learned dictionary with concept-similarity matrix C\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n} is

P(C) := \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\Bigl(1 - \max_{\ell} c_{i\ell}^2\Bigr)\ \in [0,1],

the average spread of each feature across ground-truth concepts. A feature aligned with a single concept contributes 0; one split evenly across concepts contributes near 1.

The RDP function. Collecting the three budgets, we study R^*(D_0,P_0) := \inf_\theta \{\, R(\theta) : D(\theta)\le D_0,\ P(\theta)\le P_0 \,\}, the smallest rate achievable under a joint distortion and polysemanticity budget. This is the SAE analogue of Shannon’s rate-distortion function with one extra constraint.

What does a dictionary actually look like at the two ends of the polysemanticity axis? The same handful of activations can be explained two ways. A polysemantic dictionary lets a few features each carry several concepts at once — when, say, cat, dog, fur, and paw tend to co-occur, two features can pack all four, keeping the rate low at the cost of a high P. A monosemantic dictionary insists that each feature track exactly one concept, driving P \to 0 but forcing four features on instead of two. The second option is more interpretable but simply needs more features active — which is precisely the rate the next section quantifies.

Theory: tightening one budget pushes up the others

The central object is the RDP function R^*(D_0,P_0) defined in the margin. Two results characterize its shape.

Monotonicity. R^*(D_0,P_0) is nonincreasing in P_0: demanding more monosemantic features (a smaller P_0) can only raise the required rate at a fixed distortion. Tightening any one of the three budgets forces at least one of the other two up.

A rate tax for monosemanticity. Let an optimal polysemantic baseline reach distortion D_\infty with rate k. Any SAE that matches that distortion while being monosemantic must pay

R(\theta) \ \ge\ \mathbb{E}_{p_c}\lVert c\rVert_0 - \delta \ >\ k,

where \mathbb{E}_{p_c}\lVert c\rVert_0 is the expected number of co-occurring concepts and \delta a small slack. The premium is governed entirely by how concepts co-occur in the data: when concepts fire together, a monosemantic code must spend a separate feature on each, while a polysemantic code can reuse one.

Under an orthogonality assumption the loss collapses to a clean combinatorial form, \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_S}\!\left[\,|S| + |\hat S_\theta| - 2\,|S\cap\hat S_\theta|\,\right], the expected symmetric difference between true and reconstructed concept sets — making the rate tax explicit as the cost of resolving every co-occurrence.

The rate tax, made tangible

Read the bound as a dial on the polysemanticity budget P_0. As P_0 tightens from permissive toward strict monosemanticity, the optimal operating point must climb the rate axis to hold distortion fixed at D_0: it can no longer pack co-occurring concepts into a shared feature, so it spends a separate feature on each. The vertical gap that opens above the polysemantic optimum — the floor at rate k — is the rate tax, and the dual statement holds on the distortion axis. Both taxes share the same driver: the conservation law D + R = \mathbb{E}_{p_c}\lVert c\rVert_0 on the monosemantic class, which is why the price is set entirely by how often concepts co-occur in the data.

Results

The predicted frontier is a staircase: at a fixed polysemanticity budget the optimal rate steps up as the distortion budget tightens, and the whole monosemantic curve sits above the polysemantic one by the rate tax. The paper measures exactly this on synthetic data (Figure 1).

No widely adopted metric clearly tracks the RDP-predicted direction, and the strongest-looking proxies point the wrong way — a caution for studies that read monosemanticity off these scores.

Conclusion

Polysemanticity in SAEs is, at bottom, a property of the data: when concepts co-occur, monosemantic features cost extra rate and extra distortion, and no amount of tuning removes that tax (Elhage et al. 2022). The practical implication is that better interpretability will come from architectural and optimization choices that account for co-occurrence structure, not from chasing monosemanticity as a free objective.
